# Appendix D: AI Environmental Impact Calculations

The rapid proliferation and increasing complexity of Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems have brought to light their significant environmental footprint. From the energy-intensive training of large models to the continuous power demands of inference at scale, AI's environmental impact is a growing concern. This appendix details the technical metrics and calculations used to assess this impact.

### Energy Consumption Metrics

Understanding AI's energy demands requires specific metrics:

-   **FLOPS (floating-point operations per second):** FLOPS measure the number of floating-point computations an AI model performs. This is a fundamental metric for quantifying the computational workload of training and inference. As models grow, their FLOPS requirements can reach billions or trillions per second, directly correlating with energy consumption. For example, training a large language model can involve zettaFLOPS (10^21) of operations over its lifetime.
-   **Training compute requirements by model size:** The energy consumed during the training phase of an AI model scales superlinearly with model size (number of parameters). Training a transformer model like GPT-3, for instance, has been estimated to consume energy equivalent to tens or hundreds of thousands of pounds of CO2 emissions. These calculations involve multiplying the total FLOPS for a training run by the energy efficiency of the hardware (e.g., Joules per FLOP for GPUs or TPUs) and then converting that energy into CO2 equivalents based on the power source.
-   **Inference costs at scale:** While training is a one-time event (per version), inference (running the trained model to make predictions or generate text) occurs continuously at scale. A single query to a large model might seem negligible, but when multiplied by billions of users or transactions, the cumulative energy consumption becomes substantial. Cloud providers track inference FLOPs and associate them with specific hardware, allowing for energy estimations.
-   **Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) in data centers:** PUE is a metric used to determine the energy efficiency of a data center. It is calculated by dividing the total power entering the data center by the power delivered to the computing equipment. A PUE of 1.0 means all energy goes to compute, while in reality, PUEs typically range from 1.2 to 2.0, indicating significant energy loss in cooling, lighting, and other infrastructure. Accounting for PUE is crucial for accurate overall energy consumption estimates.

### Carbon Footprint Analysis

Translating energy consumption into environmental impact requires considering the source of electricity and other factors:

-   **Regional electricity grid composition:** The carbon footprint of AI operations heavily depends on the energy mix of the data center's location. A data center running on hydropower will have a much lower carbon footprint than one powered by coal. Carbon intensity (grams of CO2 equivalent per kilowatt-hour, gCO2eq/kWh) varies significantly by region and time of day. Researchers use grid-level carbon intensity data to convert energy consumption into CO2 emissions.
-   **Water consumption for cooling (liters per query):** Large data centers generate immense heat and require significant cooling, often relying on water-intensive evaporative cooling systems. Estimating water usage involves metrics like Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE) or Power Usage Effectiveness for Water (PUEw). Some reports estimate thousands of liters of water consumed for training a single large LLM, or for every few dozen conversational queries, highlighting an often-overlooked environmental cost.
-   **Hardware lifecycle assessment:** The environmental impact of AI extends beyond operational energy. The manufacturing, transportation, and eventual disposal of AI hardware (GPUs, servers, networking equipment) also contribute significantly to the carbon footprint, consuming rare earth minerals and energy. Lifecycle assessments (LCAs) are used to quantify these embodied emissions, from raw material extraction to end-of-life recycling or landfill.
-   **Comparison with other industries:** To contextualize AI's environmental impact, it's often compared to other sectors. For example, the training of some large models has been equated to the lifetime emissions of several cars, or the annual emissions of entire households. These comparisons help to illustrate the scale of AI's environmental challenges and inform public discourse and policy around sustainable AI development.
