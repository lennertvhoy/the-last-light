## 3. Topic Summary
This presentation argues that the next meaningful leap in AI is not better voices, avatars, or chatbots, but systems that act proactively: agents that observe signals in the world, filter them through a personal model of the user, and initiate contact when something seems relevant. Using a consent-based "Mikey-inspired" demo, it contrasts reactive chatbots with proactive social attention agents, maps the underlying attention loop, and surfaces governance risks such as impersonation, manipulation, emotional dependency, and unclear accountability under the EU AI Act.

## 4. Target Audience
- AI practitioners, product managers, and designers building or evaluating agentic AI systems.
- Teams interested in human-in-the-loop AI, governance, and responsible deployment.
- Professionals attending BoostMeUp trainings/workshops (the deck promotes follow-up education).
- General audience curious about digital twins, AI companions, and proactive AI.

## 5. Key Takeaways
- Chatbots are fundamentally reactive; the real shift is toward AI that observes, filters, scores, times, and initiates contact on its own.
- Proactive social attention — "this is something for you" — is what makes an agent feel like a presence rather than a tool.
- The building blocks (scheduled actions, agents + tools, memory/persona, voice/avatar) already exist; the breakthrough is combining them into a socially relevant loop.
- Consent, disclosure, and labeling as AI-simulation must come before any "digital double" demo.
- Human-in-the-loop approval is presented as a safe, explainable MVP for proactive agents.
- Governance risks include impersonation, manipulation, emotional dependency, filter bubbles, and unclear liability.
- The relevant legal framing is the EU AI Act, specifically autonomous decision-making systems.
- The right question is not "Is it human?" but "Real enough for what purpose?"
- A digital twin/agent can be functionally equivalent within a bounded role (sparring partner, trainer, negotiation simulator, personal news filter) without being a person.
- The presentation ends with a call to action to follow BoostMeUp trainings/workshops on building safe, explainable AI agents.

## 6. The Last Light Themes
- **Human agency vs. outsourcing:** The deck explicitly asks what we should delegate to AI and what remains human (consent, approval, accountability).
- **Conscious delegation:** Human-in-the-loop approval, audit trails, and context-bound boundaries are framed as essential, not optional.
- **State Driven Development (StateDD):** The attention loop (observe → filter → score → choose timing → send/wait) and the governance checklist mirror StateDD's emphasis on explicit state, verifiable transitions, and human oversight.
- **AI collaboration:** The agent is not a replacement but a function — sparring partner, trainer, simulator, filter.
- **Staying human when everything can be outsourced:** It distinguishes human personhood (body, responsibility, relationships, vulnerability) from functional AI roles.
- **Relevance and filter bubbles:** Raises the risk that the same personal filtering that feels caring can also narrow perspective.
